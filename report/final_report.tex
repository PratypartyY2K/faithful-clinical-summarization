\begin{abstract}
Clinical summarization has the potential to reduce documentation burden, but abstractive models can produce fluent summaries that contain unsupported or contradicted medical facts. This risk is especially serious for clinical text, where an incorrect medication, dosage, diagnosis, or follow-up instruction can be harmful even if the summary appears coherent. This project studies faithful clinical summarization through the research question proposed at the start of the project: compared with traditional end-to-end metrics, does hallucination detection improve when a generated clinical summary is decomposed into atomic claims and each claim is independently verified against the source document? We implement a two-stage framework over MIMIC-III discharge summaries. The first stage trains and evaluates a clinical summarizer from discharge-note sections. The second stage decomposes generated summaries into atomic claims and prepares a natural language inference style verifier for claim-level support prediction. The implemented summarization pipeline produces MIMIC-derived source-target examples, fine-tunes summarization models, and evaluates generated summaries using ROUGE and BERTScore. A smoke evaluation on 20 held-out examples obtains ROUGE-1 of 0.2691, ROUGE-2 of 0.1147, ROUGE-L of 0.1934, and BERTScore F1 of 0.8441. The claim extraction and verifier infrastructure are implemented, but full verifier training on MIMIC-derived claims remains future work because claim-level labels have not yet been generated. The study therefore provides an implemented foundation for the proposed research question and shows why overlap metrics alone are insufficient for the final faithfulness objective.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models can automate parts of clinical documentation, including the generation of discharge summaries and SOAP-style notes. This use case is attractive because clinicians spend substantial time writing and reviewing documentation. However, clinical summarization differs from open-domain summarization because factual errors can have direct safety implications. A generated summary may preserve the general topic of a note while still introducing a wrong medication, reversing whether a patient stopped a drug, or changing a dose from 50mg to 500mg. Traditional summarization metrics such as ROUGE \cite{lin2004rouge} and BERTScore \cite{zhang2020bertscore} can measure lexical or semantic similarity, but they do not reliably distinguish safe clinical summaries from clinically dangerous hallucinations.

The task studied in this project is faithful clinical summarization. The input is a MIMIC-III discharge note or a set of extracted discharge-note sections, and the output is a concise hospital-course style summary. The generation task can be viewed as conditional summarization: given a source clinical document $T$, the model generates a summary $S$. The faithfulness task asks whether each factual statement in $S$ is supported by $T$. This second question is the central motivation of the project.

The problem is not simply that clinical summaries should be fluent. A summary can be grammatical, coherent, and close to the reference while still being unsafe if it changes a negation, invents a treatment, or attaches the wrong medication to the patient. In clinical documentation, factuality is compositional: a sentence may contain several independent medical facts, and each fact must be checked separately. This motivates a representation in which summaries are not evaluated only as strings, but as collections of claims whose support can be traced back to the source note.

The original research question is:
\begin{quote}
Compared to traditional end-to-end evaluation metrics, does hallucination detection improve when a generated clinical summary is broken down into atomic claims and each one is independently verified against the source document?
\end{quote}

To investigate this question, we design a two-stage pipeline. First, a clinical summarizer generates a discharge-style summary from source note sections. Second, the generated summary is decomposed into independent atomic claims. Each claim is then intended to be evaluated by a natural language inference (NLI) verifier that predicts whether the source entails, contradicts, or does not provide enough evidence for the claim. Table~\ref{tab:motivation} shows the motivating example from the project proposal: a standard summary can achieve high surface similarity while reversing a clinically important fact.

This decomposition also makes the evaluation more interpretable. Instead of telling a clinician that a generated summary has a single score, the verifier can identify which claim is unsupported and what type of error occurred. For example, a generated summary could be mostly correct but contain one unsupported medication instruction. A summary-level metric may dilute that error, whereas a claim-level metric preserves it. The intended output of the verification layer is therefore both quantitative and diagnostic: it supports aggregate hallucination rates and individual error inspection.

The implemented system currently supports the summarization stage, MIMIC-III preprocessing, automatic overlap evaluation, atomic claim extraction, and the verifier architecture. The final end-to-end claim verification experiment is not yet complete because MIMIC-derived claim labels are still missing. The study therefore presents the completed summarization pipeline, the claim-verification design, the preliminary evaluation results, and the remaining steps required to answer the research question fully.

The scope of the final study is honest but still aligned with the proposal. The summarizer can be trained and evaluated, the atomic verification framework is specified and partially operational, and the main unresolved question is empirical rather than conceptual: the system still needs labeled source-claim pairs to train and validate the verifier on MIMIC-III. This distinction is important because the incomplete component is exactly the supervision needed for the proposed comparison between overlap metrics and atomic hallucination detection.

\paragraph{Hypotheses.}
The research question leads to three hypotheses. First, overlap metrics will be weak indicators of clinical faithfulness because they reward lexical or semantic similarity rather than evidence support. Second, atomic decomposition will improve hallucination detection because it evaluates the smallest clinically meaningful factual units rather than whole summaries or whole sentences. Third, claim-level error categories will make evaluation more actionable by identifying whether errors arise from medications, dosages, diagnoses, findings, or follow-up instructions. These hypotheses guide both the implemented summarizer evaluation and the planned verifier evaluation.

\paragraph{Summary of contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item We construct a MIMIC-III discharge summarization workflow that extracts narrative source-target pairs from noisy clinical notes and supports model training and evaluation.
    \item We implement a generate-then-verify framework that decomposes generated summaries into atomic medical claims and prepares an NLI-style verifier for support prediction.
    \item We provide preliminary summarizer results and identify the key remaining bottleneck: claim-level supervision is required before atomic verification can be evaluated against traditional metrics.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Abstractive summarization.}
Modern abstractive summarization is built on Transformer-based sequence modeling \cite{vaswani2017attention}. Models such as BART \cite{lewis2020bart}, PEGASUS \cite{zhang2020pegasus}, and T5 \cite{raffel2020exploring} showed that large pretrained encoder-decoder models can be adapted effectively to summarization tasks. These models optimize fluency and content coverage, but they are not inherently constrained to generate only source-supported facts.

The distinction between extractive and abstractive summarization is especially relevant in clinical text. Extractive methods can be safer because copied phrases are more likely to be grounded, but they may produce verbose summaries and fail to synthesize a patient trajectory. Abstractive methods can produce concise and readable summaries, but they introduce the risk of generating facts that are not explicitly supported by the source. This project assumes that abstractive generation is useful for clinical documentation, but only if paired with a faithfulness mechanism that can detect unsupported facts after generation.

\paragraph{Clinical summarization.}
Clinical summarization has been studied in several settings, including doctor-patient conversation summarization and hospital-course summarization. \citet{krishna2021soap} proposed modular summarization for SOAP note generation from clinical conversations, showing that decomposing clinical documentation into sub-problems can improve controllability. \citet{adams2021hospitalcourse} studied hospital-course summarization and highlighted the difficulty of generating concise summaries from long and heterogeneous clinical documentation. \citet{landes2023dsprov} analyzed provenance issues in discharge summarization and showed that clinical source-summary alignment is often noisy. Recent BioNLP shared-task systems have explored instruction-tuned models, context selection, and LoRA-style adaptation for discharge-summary generation \cite{wu2024epfl, he2024shimo, liu2024csiro}. These studies motivate the use of MIMIC-derived discharge notes but also show that clinical summarization requires careful preprocessing.

One lesson from this literature is that the definition of the source document matters. A discharge note may contain several sections that are not equally useful for generating a hospital-course summary. Medication lists, discharge instructions, and signatures are important for the full discharge document, but they may not be appropriate targets for a narrative hospital-course summarizer. For this reason, the present data pipeline emphasizes clinically informative narrative sections rather than treating the entire note as an undifferentiated sequence.

\paragraph{Factuality and hallucination detection.}
Factual consistency has become a major limitation of abstractive summarization. \citet{maynez2020faithfulness} showed that abstractive systems can hallucinate content despite reasonable ROUGE scores. \citet{kryscinski2020evaluating} framed factual consistency as a separate evaluation problem, while later work used question answering, entailment, and learned classifiers to evaluate factuality \cite{wang2020asking, scialom2021questeval, goyal2021annotating, laban2022summac, fabbri2022qafacteval}. FactKB \cite{feng2023factkb} and FENICE \cite{scire2024fenice} further demonstrate that factuality evaluation benefits from explicit modeling rather than only text overlap.

NLI-based approaches are attractive because they directly model whether a hypothesis follows from a premise. In this project, the source note acts as the premise and each atomic claim acts as the hypothesis. However, clinical NLI has additional challenges: source documents are long, evidence may be distributed across sections, and medical abbreviations can change meaning depending on context. These challenges suggest that claim verification should be treated as a clinical NLP component rather than as a generic off-the-shelf entailment problem.

\paragraph{Atomic decomposition.}
The most directly relevant line of work is atomic factual evaluation. FActScore \cite{min2023factscore} decomposes long-form generations into atomic facts and measures how many are supported by evidence. SelfCheckGPT \cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} detects hallucinations by measuring inconsistency across sampled generations. These methods were largely developed for open-domain long-form generation, such as biographies, but the same idea is well suited to clinical summarization. A single sentence in a clinical summary may contain multiple facts: diagnosis, medication, dose, frequency, and disposition. Sentence-level verification may miss errors if most of the sentence is correct but one atom is wrong.

The project differs from open-domain factuality work in its error priorities. In a biography, an unsupported date or affiliation may reduce factual precision. In medicine, an unsupported treatment or medication change can create clinical risk. The goal is therefore not only to compute a factuality score, but also to produce error categories that are meaningful to clinical review: medications, dosages, diagnoses, lab findings, symptoms, and follow-up instructions.

\paragraph{Efficient adaptation and clinical language models.}
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods such as LoRA \cite{hu2022lora} and QLoRA \cite{dettmers2023qlora} make it practical to adapt larger instruction-following models under limited compute. Biomedical and clinical pretrained models also show the importance of domain adaptation for medical language \cite{alsentzer2019publicly, gu2021domain}. For the verifier stage, DeBERTa \cite{he2021deberta} is a natural NLI backbone because it performs strongly on language understanding and entailment-style tasks.

This project combines these threads. The generation stage follows clinical summarization work, while the evaluation goal follows atomic factuality work. The proposed contribution is to apply atomic claim verification to clinical summaries, where the cost of unsupported facts is high and overlap metrics are inadequate.

\section{Data}

The primary dataset is MIMIC-III \cite{johnson2016mimic}, a large de-identified critical care database containing structured records and free-text notes. The project focuses on discharge summaries from the \texttt{NOTEEVENTS} table. MIMIC-III is appropriate for this research question because it contains realistic clinical language, long documents, abbreviations, formatting irregularities, and clinician-authored discharge narratives. These properties make the task more realistic than synthetic summarization data.

The raw notes are not directly formatted as summarization examples, so the data pipeline constructs source-target pairs. Each discharge note is parsed into sections using heading patterns. Source text is drawn from clinically informative sections such as chief complaint, history of present illness, physical exam, and pertinent results. The target summary is selected from narrative hospital-course content, especially the brief hospital course. Highly structured sections such as discharge medications and discharge instructions are excluded from the target because they often consist of long lists and boilerplate rather than coherent narrative summaries.

This preprocessing step is necessary because raw MIMIC notes contain formatting artifacts, de-identification spans, dictation metadata, inconsistent headings, and repeated boilerplate. Treating all text equally would make the learning problem noisier and could encourage the model to generate long lists instead of concise summaries. The section-based construction instead defines a more focused task: summarize the clinically relevant admission and hospital information into a short narrative hospital-course summary.

The target selection is intentionally conservative. Brief hospital course sections are more likely to contain the kind of concise clinical trajectory that the model should learn. In contrast, discharge medication sections are often enumerated lists of drug names, doses, and frequencies. Those fields are clinically important, but they are also high-risk for hallucination and may require specialized structured extraction rather than free-form summarization. Excluding them from the generation target reduces one source of noise in the first-stage summarizer while leaving medication-related hallucination analysis as an important future extension.

The input-output design also differs from the original proposal in one practical respect. The proposal described pairing aggregated daily care notes with discharge summaries. In the current study, the source is constructed from discharge-note sections because those sections are available in a consistent raw table and can be paired with hospital-course targets without requiring cross-note temporal aggregation. This still preserves the core research question: the model generates a clinical summary from source clinical text, and the verifier should decide whether generated claims are supported by that source. Future work can extend the same framework to longer multi-note inputs.

The resulting example format is shown in Table~\ref{tab:dataset}. The source is a set of note sections, the target is a concise hospital-course summary, and metadata records the original admission identifiers and selected section names. The same raw examples are intended to support both the summarization task and the verifier task. For the verifier task, however, each generated or reference summary must also be decomposed into labeled atomic claims.

The current implemented dataset supports summarizer training and evaluation. The claim-verification dataset is not yet complete because atomic claims have not been labeled against the source documents. This means that the summarization part of the study can be evaluated empirically, while the final atomic verification hypothesis remains partially untested.

The intended verifier dataset will contain one row per source-claim pair. Positive examples should be claims that are directly supported by the source document. Contradiction examples should include claims that conflict with the source, such as reversed medication status, incorrect dosage, or negated symptoms. Neutral examples should include plausible clinical claims that are not stated in the source. Constructing this dataset is more difficult than ordinary summary preprocessing because labels must be grounded in clinical evidence, not merely in textual overlap with the reference summary.

\paragraph{Claim labeling strategy.}
A complete claim-labeled dataset should combine supported claims from reference summaries with difficult negative examples. Supported claims can be extracted from clinician-written hospital-course summaries and checked against the source. Contradiction examples can be created by controlled perturbations, such as changing a medication status from stopped to continued, altering a dosage, reversing a symptom negation, or substituting a diagnosis. Neutral examples can be generated by adding plausible but unstated clinical facts. This strategy would produce training data that targets the exact hallucination types most relevant to clinical summarization.

The labeling process should also preserve evidence spans when possible. A support label is more trustworthy if it can be linked to the sentence or section that justifies the claim. Evidence spans would allow error analysis to distinguish between verifier failure and missing evidence. They would also make the evaluation more transparent because a reviewer could inspect why a claim was judged supported or unsupported. Although span-level evidence is not required for the first verifier model, it would improve the scientific quality of the dataset.

\paragraph{Data quality risks.}
MIMIC-III discharge notes are realistic, but they are not perfect supervision. A reference summary may include information from sections that are not included in the extracted source, or it may omit details that are present in the note. This creates apparent unsupported claims even when the clinician summary is valid in the full medical record. For this reason, verifier labels should be interpreted relative to the selected source context. Future work should either expand the source context or annotate whether missing evidence is due to genuine hallucination or incomplete source extraction.

\paragraph{Generative summarizer.}
The summarization model is trained to estimate $P(S \mid T)$, where $T$ is the source clinical document and $S$ is the target summary. The implementation supports both encoder-decoder summarizers, such as FLAN-T5, and decoder-only instruction-tuned causal language models. The main adaptation path follows the project proposal by supporting QLoRA fine-tuning for an instruction-tuned large language model. The model receives a clinical documentation prompt that asks it to produce a concise faithful summary grounded only in the source.

The prompt is designed to reduce unsupported generation by explicitly instructing the model to ground the summary in the source. Prompting alone is not expected to solve hallucination, but it provides a simple first control. The training objective then teaches the model to map source note sections to the clinician-authored hospital-course target. For causal language models, the prompt and target are concatenated and only target tokens contribute to the loss. For encoder-decoder models, the source prompt is encoded and the target summary is decoded.

The use of QLoRA is motivated by computational constraints. Full fine-tuning of an 8B parameter model is expensive, whereas low-rank adapters allow the model to specialize to clinical summarization with a smaller trainable parameter set. This design matches the proposal's goal of adapting an open-source LLM without requiring full model training.

\paragraph{Atomic claim extractor.}
After generation, the summary is decomposed into atomic claims. A claim is intended to contain one independently checkable medical fact, such as a diagnosis, medication change, symptom, treatment, or follow-up instruction. The current implementation includes a heuristic extractor based on sentence and clause segmentation, plus an optional LLM-based extractor that returns independent medical facts in structured form. Atomic decomposition is necessary because one generated sentence can mix supported and unsupported facts.

For example, the sentence ``The patient was treated with steroids and discharged on Lisinopril'' contains at least two claims: the treatment with steroids and the discharge medication. If the source supports the steroid treatment but says the patient stopped Lisinopril, sentence-level evaluation may obscure the error. Atomic decomposition makes the unit of evaluation closer to the unit of clinical risk.

\paragraph{Claim verifier.}
The verifier is formulated as NLI over a source document and a candidate claim:
\[
f(T, C) \rightarrow \{\text{entailed}, \text{contradicted}, \text{neutral}\},
\]
where $T$ is the source document and $C$ is an atomic claim. A DeBERTa-style sequence classifier is used as the intended verifier backbone. A supported claim corresponds to entailment; a hallucination corresponds to contradiction or lack of support, depending on the claim and evidence. This design produces interpretable claim-level outputs rather than a single opaque score for the whole summary.

The verifier is also useful as an analysis tool even before it is used to improve generation. If many unsupported claims are medication-related, then future modeling should focus on medication grounding. If most unsupported claims are neutral rather than contradicted, then the generator may be adding plausible but unstated details. If contradictions dominate, the model may be mishandling negation or temporal changes. Thus, claim verification supports both evaluation and targeted model improvement.

\paragraph{Using verification for correction.}
Although this project focuses on evaluation, the same verifier could eventually support self-correction. A generated summary could be decomposed into claims, unsupported claims could be removed or revised, and a final summary could be reconstructed from supported content. This would convert the verifier from a passive evaluator into an active safety layer. Such a correction stage is beyond the present experiments, but it motivates the long-term value of atomic verification.

\section{Experiment Setups}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The evaluation plan follows the proposal by combining overlap metrics with faithfulness metrics. ROUGE \cite{lin2004rouge} measures n-gram overlap with the reference summary, and BERTScore \cite{zhang2020bertscore} measures semantic similarity using contextual embeddings. These metrics are useful as summarization baselines but do not directly answer whether the generated facts are supported by the source.

The primary intended faithfulness metric is a FActScore-style claim support rate \cite{min2023factscore}. Given a set of atomic claims $A$ extracted from a generated summary, the score is:
\[
\mathrm{SupportRate}(S,T) = \frac{1}{|A|}\sum_{a \in A}\mathbb{I}[\mathrm{supported}(a,T)].
\]
The same claim predictions can also produce contradiction rate and neutral-or-unsupported rate. These metrics are more aligned with the research question because they directly evaluate whether individual generated facts are grounded in the source.

Human evaluation was proposed as an additional validation step, with reviewers judging fluency, coherence, and faithfulness for a small set of summaries. This remains a desirable extension because clinical factuality is high stakes, but it has not yet been completed.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

The summarizer is trained on processed MIMIC-III source-target examples. The causal language model setting uses parameter-efficient fine-tuning with LoRA or QLoRA, with low-rank adapters applied to attention and feed-forward projection layers. The seq2seq baseline setting supports FLAN-T5 style training. The summarizer input length and target length are configurable; the strongest current setting uses longer source contexts for the causal model and generates summaries up to 128--192 new tokens.

For preliminary evaluation, generation uses greedy decoding or small-batch decoding with configurable repetition and length controls. The reported experiments are smoke evaluations over 20 held-out examples. This scale is sufficient to verify that the summarization pipeline functions end-to-end, but not sufficient for strong statistical claims.

The verifier training protocol is designed but not fully executed. The missing step is generation of claim-level labels for MIMIC summaries. Once labeled examples are available, the verifier can be trained on source-claim pairs and evaluated using accuracy, macro-F1, precision, recall, and claim-level support metrics.

The planned experimental comparison has three levels. First, summarizers are compared using ROUGE and BERTScore to measure conventional generation quality. Second, generated summaries are decomposed into claims and evaluated by the verifier to compute support and hallucination rates. Third, examples are manually inspected to determine whether the claim-level metric identifies clinically meaningful errors that are not obvious from overlap scores. The central hypothesis is that the third level will reveal errors hidden by the first level.

The final metric comparison should be analyzed at both the system level and the example level. At the system level, a model with higher BERTScore but lower claim support rate would demonstrate the limitation of semantic similarity metrics. At the example level, individual summaries can be ranked by BERTScore and claim support rate to find disagreements. These disagreement cases are the most informative evidence for the research question because they show when traditional evaluation and atomic verification lead to different conclusions.

An ablation study is also natural for this framework. The claim extractor can be replaced with sentence-level verification to test whether atomic decomposition improves error localization. The verifier can be evaluated with binary labels versus three-way NLI labels to test whether distinguishing contradiction from neutral improves clinical interpretability. Finally, the summarizer prompt can be evaluated with and without explicit grounding instructions to test whether prompting reduces unsupported claims before verification.

\paragraph{Success criteria.}
The project should not be judged only by whether the summarizer obtains the highest ROUGE score. A successful faithful summarization system should generate readable summaries, avoid unsupported clinical facts, and provide evidence when a summary is unsafe. In the final evaluation, success would mean that atomic verification detects hallucinations that are not reflected in ROUGE or BERTScore, and that the detected errors correspond to clinically meaningful categories. If two summaries have similar BERTScore but different claim support rates, the claim-level metric is providing information that conventional metrics miss.

For this reason, the most important final table would not only report ROUGE and BERTScore. It should include average number of claims per summary, claim support rate, contradiction rate, neutral-or-unsupported rate, and representative hallucination categories. This table would directly connect the generation model to the clinical safety objective. A model that generates fewer but better-supported claims may be preferable to a model that generates longer, more detailed summaries with a higher hallucination rate.

These results show that the generation stage is operational, but they do not fully answer the research question. ROUGE and BERTScore summarize similarity to the reference summary, not factual support against the source document. A generated summary could obtain a reasonable BERTScore while still containing a contradicted medication status or incorrect dosage. Therefore, these metrics should be treated as baselines for generation quality, not as evidence of clinical faithfulness.

The relative values are consistent with the difficulty of the task. ROUGE-2 is substantially lower than ROUGE-1, suggesting that the generated text does not often match reference phrasing at the bigram level. This is expected for abstractive clinical summarization, where there may be multiple valid ways to summarize a hospital course. BERTScore F1 is higher, indicating semantic similarity even when lexical overlap is limited. However, the gap between lexical and semantic metrics reinforces the need for factual evaluation: semantic similarity can remain high when a small but important clinical detail is wrong.

The main analysis from the current stage is that the project has reached the point where the proposed comparison can be made, but the claim-level labels required for the verifier are still missing. The atomic claim extractor can produce candidate facts from generated summaries, and the verifier architecture can score source-claim pairs once trained. However, without labeled MIMIC-derived claims, claim support rate cannot yet be reported as a reliable metric.

The preliminary results should therefore be interpreted as a baseline rather than as a final answer. They establish that the summarizer can generate outputs from the constructed MIMIC examples, and they provide overlap scores against which later verifier-based metrics can be compared. If future claim verification shows that summaries with similar ROUGE or BERTScore have different hallucination rates, that would directly support the proposal's argument that traditional metrics are incomplete for clinical summarization.

\paragraph{Expected qualitative behavior.}
The most important future examples will be cases where the generated summary is mostly correct but contains one high-risk unsupported claim. Medication continuation and discontinuation are especially important because they often depend on temporal language. Negation is another likely source of error: a source may say that a patient denied chest pain, while a generated summary may simply mention chest pain. Numeric values are also high-risk because a small surface change can be clinically large. These examples are exactly where atomic claim verification should be more informative than whole-summary scoring.

\paragraph{Implications.}
The preliminary study suggests that faithful clinical summarization should be treated as a pipeline problem rather than only a generation problem. Improving the generator may reduce hallucinations, but evaluation must still expose unsupported facts. The atomic verifier provides a mechanism for doing so in a way that can be inspected by humans. Even if the verifier is imperfect, it changes the evaluation unit from a summary string to a set of medical propositions, which better matches the clinical risk profile of the task.

The main scientific value of the current stage is therefore methodological. The project formalizes how to move from summary-level similarity to claim-level support in clinical summarization. The preliminary numbers show that the generation component is viable, while the incomplete verifier labels identify the next necessary experiment. This is a useful outcome because it prevents overclaiming based on ROUGE and BERTScore alone. In a high-stakes domain, recognizing what has not yet been validated is part of a faithful evaluation methodology.

This limitation is directly connected to the research question rather than separate from it. The hypothesis is that atomic verification will reveal hallucinations that overlap metrics miss. To test that hypothesis rigorously, future experiments must compare summaries using both metric families on the same examples: ROUGE/BERTScore at the summary level and entailment-based support rates at the claim level. The expected outcome is that some summaries with acceptable overlap scores will contain unsupported claims, especially in clinically sensitive categories such as medications, dosages, diagnoses, and follow-up instructions.

\paragraph{Error analysis plan.}
Once verifier outputs are available, unsupported claims can be grouped into clinically meaningful categories: medication errors, dosage or frequency errors, diagnosis errors, lab or finding errors, symptom errors, and follow-up errors. This analysis will show not only whether hallucinations occur, but what types of hallucinations are most common. Such a breakdown is more useful for clinical summarization than a single aggregate score.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

The most important limitation is that the full atomic verification experiment has not yet been completed. The current system can train and evaluate summarizers and can extract atomic claims, but MIMIC-derived claim labels are not yet available. Therefore, verifier training and final claim-level faithfulness evaluation remain future work.

Several steps are required to complete the study. First, atomic claims must be generated from reference summaries, generated summaries, or both. Second, each claim must be labeled as entailed, contradicted, or neutral with respect to the source document. This labeling could be manual, LLM-assisted, based on synthetic perturbations, or a hybrid of these strategies. Third, the DeBERTa verifier must be trained and evaluated on non-empty claim-labeled splits. Fourth, the final comparison should evaluate whether claim-level support metrics detect clinically meaningful hallucinations that ROUGE and BERTScore fail to penalize.

Another limitation is the scale of the preliminary evaluation. The reported results are based on 20 held-out examples, so they should be interpreted as a functional smoke test rather than as final benchmark results. A complete final experiment should evaluate on a larger held-out set and include comparisons among at least one seq2seq baseline, one instruction-tuned causal model, and the atomic verification pipeline.

Finally, human evaluation remains important. Automatic claim verification can identify many unsupported statements, but clinical correctness often depends on context and nuance. A small blinded human evaluation of generated summaries would provide a stronger validation of both the summarizer and the verifier.

\section{Conclusion}

This project studies faithful clinical summarization through the lens of atomic claim verification. The motivating question is whether decomposing a generated clinical summary into independently verifiable claims can detect hallucinations better than traditional end-to-end metrics. The implemented work establishes the first stage of this study: MIMIC-III discharge notes are converted into summarization examples, clinical summaries can be generated, and preliminary ROUGE and BERTScore results can be computed. The system also implements the core structure required for the second stage: atomic claim extraction and an NLI-style verifier design.

The current evidence supports one clear conclusion: summary-level overlap metrics are not sufficient for the intended clinical faithfulness goal. They provide useful generation baselines, but they cannot determine whether each medical claim is supported by the source. The remaining work is to populate the verifier dataset with labeled atomic claims and run the full generate-then-verify evaluation. Once this step is complete, the project can directly test the original hypothesis that atomic claim verification reveals clinically important hallucinations that traditional metrics miss.
